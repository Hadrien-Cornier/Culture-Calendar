    9 / 10

    Raiders of the Lost Ark

    Paramount · Film

    • Wed, Jun 10 · 7:30 PM

    Spielberg's kinetic adventure: geography-clear action, Williams's propulsive score, Ford's weary heroism.

    🎭 Artistic Merit, Spielberg and Lucas make danger feel physical at every turn. The truck chase, the boulder escape, the airplane propeller kill, and the burning warehouse all read cleanly, with action staged so the eye never loses the geography. John Williams’s score does more than decorate the film, it gives the hero a hard, brass-edged profile and turns each setback into a charge forward. The performances are crisp, with Harrison Ford selling fatigue, irritation, and instinctive bravado in the same beat.

    ✨ Originality, the film is built from older materials, jungle serials, pulp archaeology, war adventure, but it recombines them with unusual speed and confidence. Its real invention is tonal control, it can jump from gag to threat to genuine menace without breaking momentum. The result is not novel in subject, but it is unusually agile in form, and that agility keeps familiar parts from feeling stale.

    📚 Cultural Significance, this is one of the defining adventure films of the modern era. It set a template for blockbuster pacing, for how to make a hero readable in motion, and for how to turn set pieces into shared cultural memory. Its influence is visible in decades of action cinema, from treasure hunts to franchise filmmaking, and it still functions as a common reference point for audience pleasure.

    💡 Intellectual Depth, the film is not built for philosophy, but it has sharper concerns than a simple chase picture. It treats history as something people loot, weaponize, and misread, and it places superstition, power, and colonial appetite in the same frame. The Ark itself becomes a test of human vanity, with the climactic destruction punishing those who try to seize sacred force as property.

  2. 02
    9 / 10

    DAISIES

    AFS · Film

    • Wed, Jun 10 · 8:30 PM

    Anarchic 1960s provocation drowns formal innovation in repetitive destruction and gimmickry.

    🎭 Artistic Merit

    Věra Chytilová's craft deploys rapid cuts between color filters—black-and-white to green, pink, blue—in the banquet sequence, where two young women, Marie I and Marie II, smash platters of butter, sausages, and cakes; this technical assault on film stock yields a chaotic montage that overloads the frame with flying debris and splintered objects, achieving sensory overload through handheld shots and jump cuts. Execution peaks in the scissors duel, blades snipping at hair and fabric amid distorted lenses, while the soundtrack layers Czech folk tunes with industrial noise; such mastery elevates the film's 76 minutes into a barrage of visual entropy unmatched in 1960s cinema.

    ✨ Originality

    Chytilová shatters narrative linearity with fourth-wall ruptures—the Marias fire guns at the audience, then pivot to typewriter pecks spelling out fragmented slogans; unpredictability surges in the butter-melting close-ups intercut with tuba blasts, dodging every coming-of-age trope for anarchic glee. No prior New Wave film fused Dadaist pranks with such optical tricks, like superimposing faces over food mountains, rendering the work a high-entropy outlier.

    📚 Cultural Significance

    Banned in Czechoslovakia until 1967 for "food wastage" amid shortages, Daisies ignited the Czech New Wave's feminist edge, influencing Godard and Anger with its girl-gang rebellion; its restoration and Criterion release cemented it as a touchstone for experimental cinema, where female directors weaponize frivolity against state dogma, contributing raw disruption to global avant-garde traditions.

    💡 Intellectual Depth

    Themes dissect bourgeois excess through the Marias' escalating capers—from bathtub banquets to decapitated doll heads—probing consumer rot and female ennui under communism; universal impulses surface in their gleeful sabotage, mirroring absurd rebellion against scripted roles, with motifs like duplicated identities questioning self and society in layered, non-didactic bursts.

    Singin' in the Rain

    Paramount · Film

    • Thu, Jun 11 · 7:30 PM

    Gene Kelly dances through rain and comedy with unstoppable physical joy.

    🎭 Artistic Merit, Gene Kelly and Stanley Donen build a machine out of rhythm, choreography, color, and timing. The title number turns rain, streetlamps, and a sidewalk into a full-bodied display of physical joy. The film also lands its comedy cleanly, especially in the voice-dubbing chaos, the diction lessons, and the musical numbers that keep shifting tone without losing control. The performances are precise, with Kelly giving the lead role a physical ease that makes the hardest work look casual.

    ✨ Originality, The movie is a sharp backstage comedy about a film industry in panic as silent pictures give way to sound. That setting gives it a built-in tension most musicals do not have. It is not unpredictable in plot, but it is inventive in form, because it turns technical filmmaking problems, microphones, recording booths, bad synchronization, into material for jokes and songs. The result is a musical that knows exactly how artificial show business is, and uses that artifice as its subject.

    📚 Cultural Significance, This is one of the defining Hollywood musicals, and its influence is obvious in how later films borrow its mix of spectacle, irony, and self-awareness. It also preserves a key moment in cinema history, the messy transition from silent film to talkies. That makes it more than a nostalgic crowd-pleaser. It is a record of the industry looking at itself and laughing, while also showing off the discipline required to make entertainment look effortless.

    💡 Intellectual Depth, Beneath the brightness, the film is about performance as labor, and about how artists survive technological change. Don Lockwood’s polished image, Lina Lamont’s collapse under the demands of sound, and Kathy Selden’s voice work all point to a central idea, talent is not always what audiences first see. The film also understands that cinema is a collaborative illusion, built from bodies, voices, timing, and engineering. That is a richer idea than the surface cheer suggests, and the movie keeps finding new ways to stage it.

    SWEET SMELL OF SUCCESS

    AFS · Film

    • Fri, Jun 12 · 7:30 PM
    • Sun, Jun 14 · 1:30 PM

    Corrosive noir about a gossip columnist's moral collapse in Manhattan's theater world.

    🎭 Artistic Merit

    Alexander Mackendrick directs with taut precision; James Wong Howe's black-and-white cinematography captures New York's nocturnal pulse through stark shadows and crowded frames, as in the opening shot of JJ Hunsecker scanning Times Square from his balcony perch. Clifford Odets's dialogue snaps like a tabloid headline—"You're a cookie full of arsenic"—delivered by Burt Lancaster's towering Hunsecker and Tony Curtis's jittery Sidney Falco; their performances lock in a vise grip, with Curtis's fidgety glances and Lancaster's icy stares driving every scene. The score by Elmer Bernstein underscores tension without excess; editing slices conversations into verbal knife-fights, peaking in the control-room climax where Falco's betrayal unravels amid flickering lights and hissed threats.

    ✨ Originality

    The film sidesteps noir tropes of lone detectives for a raw portrait of symbiotic corruption; Hunsekcer's godlike press baron manipulates Falco not through guns but columns and whispers, upending power dynamics in a script that twists press ethics into a personal vendetta. Mackendrick imports British Ealing Studios bite to Hollywood cynicism, yielding unpredictable beats like Falco's botched romance amid a crowded Automat, where small talk curdles into desperation.

    📚 Cultural Significance

    Sweet Smell of Success skewers 1950s media moguls, presaging Walter Winchell's real-life downfall; its Broadway gossip-mill dissection influenced films from Network to The Wolf of Wall Street, cementing a template for ambition's media-fueled rot. Revived in prints and AFI rankings, it endures as a snapshot of urban ambition's underbelly.

    💡 Intellectual Depth

    Themes dissect dependency's poison: Falco craves Hunsecker's favor like oxygen, their bond a mirror to patronage's hierarchies; the script probes free speech's illusion, as Hunsecker torches reputations with "the golden rule—those who have the gold make the rules." Universal in its anatomy of moral erosion, it layers personal ruin atop societal critique without sermons.

    The Virgin Suicides

    Paramount · Film

    • Mon, Jun 8 · 7:30 PM

    Coppola's soft-focus study of suburban confinement and adolescent desire, beautifully composed bu...

    ★ Rating: [8/10]

    🎭 Artistic Merit, Sofia Coppola’s film is built with severe control. The pastel suburbia, the gauzy light, and the period detail do more than recreate the 1970s, they trap the Lisbon house in a climate of boredom and surveillance. Edward Lachman’s cinematography gives the film a sealed, airless look, while the music, especially the Air score, turns ordinary teenage drift into a steady pressure. The performances are carefully keyed, with Kirsten Dunst giving Lux both flirtation and exhaustion, and the parents rendered as damaged functionaries rather than broad caricatures.

    ✨ Originality, The premise could have become a standard tragic teen story, but the film refuses easy explanation. It stays inside the boys’ memory of the sisters, then exposes how little that memory can truly know. The cryptic structure, the voiceover, and the repeated objects, records, cigarettes, the pollen-yellow trees, the locked bedroom, create a narrative of obsession rather than disclosure. That limitation is the point, and it keeps the film from sinking into ordinary melodrama.

    📚 Cultural Significance, The film became one of the defining American works about girlhood as observed, mythologized, and misunderstood. It helped establish Coppola as a major filmmaker and gave later directors a clearer model for stylized female-centered coming-of-age stories that are suspicious of male narration. Its influence is visible in the way it treats suburbia not as a safe backdrop but as a social enclosure.

    💡 Intellectual Depth, The film is strongest when it studies the gap between appearance and interior life. It asks what a community owes to its daughters, what boys think they are watching, and what grief does when it cannot be translated into knowledge. The suicides remain partly opaque, and the film gains force from that refusal to solve its own mystery. It is about desire, surveillance, isolation, and the failure of adults to read suffering before it hardens into catastrophe.

    World Wide What Book Club - Blue Dragon, White Tiger

    FirstLight · Books

    • Mon, Jun 8 · 7:00 PM

    Vietnamese American caught between communism, exile, and national loyalty.

    ★ Rating, 7/10

    Tran Van Dinh’s Blue Dragon, White Tiger has the hard, inconvenient authority of a book written close to the event and willing to sit in contradiction. The novel follows Tran Van Minh from a Massachusetts classroom back to Vietnam in 1967, then across years of war work, ideological drift, and exile, and its best quality is not tidy argument but pressure, the pressure of a man trying to keep faith with family, country, and politics at once.

    What stands out is the book’s reach. Minh does not move through history as a hero with a clean conversion, he becomes sympathetic to the Viet Cong, joins a communist sleeper cell, works at a North Vietnam command post, and later serves as a delegate in Paris. That itinerary gives the novel an uncommon vantage point on the Vietnamese war and its aftershocks, less battlefield spectacle than political thicket. The book’s title promises emblem and conflict, and Dinh delivers both through a character caught between national myth and personal dislocation.

    As a novel, it is more serious than stylish. The prose, from the evidence available, seems built to carry argument and memory rather than dazzle. That can make the book feel earnest, even schematic in places, but the seriousness is part of its value. This is the first Vietnamese American novel written in English and published in the United States, and that historical position matters, because the book reads like an early attempt to seize narrative authority from outside accounts of Vietnam and hand it to a Vietnamese voice.

    Culturally, its importance is clear. It belongs to the foundational shelf of Vietnamese American literature, and its return to print after decades out of circulation gives it a second life it deserves. Intellectually, it is most interesting when it refuses easy national loyalties and instead treats politics as lived confusion, not slogan. It is not a polished crowd-pleaser, but it is a necessary book, and one whose roughness feels earned rather than accidental.

    💡 Intellectual Depth

    Prospero's god-complex unravels against inevitable rot; themes pit aristocratic decadence against egalitarian death, with satanic rites probing faith's fragility amid plague. Juliana's self-immolation and the lovers' escape sketch fleeting humanity versus power's void, all compressed into feverish vignettes that probe mortality without sermons.

    MISHIMA: A LIFE IN FOUR CHAPTERS

    AFS · Film

    • Thu, Jun 11 · 7:00 PM

    Stylized biography fractures Mishima's life through kabuki-inflected color and monochrome artifice.

    🎭 Artistic Merit

    Schrader splits Yukio Mishima's life into four chapters, each styled as a distinct genre: black-and-white realism for his early years, Noh theater for family tensions, vivid Technicolor for his novel Runaway Horses, and stark expressionism for his 1970 seppuku. The craft shines in transitions, like the recurring sunflower motif linking biography to fiction; cinematographer John Bailey's lighting shifts from muted grays to saturated reds with precision. Performances anchor it: Ken Ogata's Mishima conveys rigid posture in military scenes, while Yasosuke Bashi's youthful vigor bursts in the riot sequence. Execution falters in pacing; the final chapter rushes the coup, diluting impact.

    ✨ Originality

    The film's structure innovates by nesting autobiography within dramatized novel excerpts, dodging linear biopic tropes; no single scene mimics Hollywood templates. Unpredictability peaks in the Kyoko's House chapter, where actors break character mid-scene to reveal Mishima's puppeteering of life as art. It sidesteps cliché through genre mashups, yet borrows Schrader's Taxi Driver obsessions with isolation and action.

    📚 Cultural Significance

    This bridges Japanese and Western cinema, adapting Mishima's nationalist fervor for American audiences via English narration; it influenced biopics like I'm Not There in fragmenting subjects. Relevance endures in debates over art's political edge, echoing Mishima's emperor worship amid Japan's post-war identity crisis. Contribution lies in visualizing his aesthetic extremism, rare for 1985.

    💡 Intellectual Depth

    Themes tangle beauty, death, and action: the Runaway Horses sequence probes samurai revival through a student's bomb plot, questioning harmony versus rupture. Universal pulls emerge in Mishima's bodybuilding montages, equating sculpted flesh to forged text. Complexity arises from contradictory drives; early literary success clashes with later militarism, unresolved.
